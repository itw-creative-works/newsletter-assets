# Open source funding is shifting again

_The old playbook is thinning out, and infra-backed deals are getting harder to ignore._

The open source funding model for VC-backed developer tools is getting harder to ignore. What used to look like a healthy mix of product, community, and growth is increasingly resolving into two outcomes: sell, or build a platform business around the project.

---

## The middle path is disappearing

![A split-path road sign at dusk showing two directions, one toward a cloud platform datacenter and one toward a small storefront labeled hosting, rendered in clean editorial illustration style.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/ultimate-jekyll/-OuUKBNEJX9MYzxYZunu/section-1.png)

For years, the story around VC-backed open source was simple enough: raise capital, grow a community, and find a business model later. That worked when there were enough wedges, enough growth, and enough room to stay independent.

Now the pattern is more brutal. The biggest projects in the category are either being absorbed by larger infrastructure companies or forced to prove a direct revenue engine, usually hosting or managed services. The result is less optionality for founders and fewer long-term independent homes for the tools developers rely on every day.

---

## Hosting is becoming the default monetization layer

![A developer dashboard transforming into a cloud control panel, with build logs and deployment cards floating into a hosting interface, modern flat illustration.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/ultimate-jekyll/-OuUKBNEJX9MYzxYZunu/section-2.png)

The most believable independent business model for many open source tools is no longer licensing or support. It is hosted infrastructure. If the project touches builds, previews, deployment, or collaboration, the monetization story writes itself: make the local tool excellent, then sell the hosted version of the workflow.

That creates a very practical flywheel, especially for Jekyll-style teams that want a simple stack. The tension is that once hosting becomes the main business, product priorities can drift away from the original developer experience and toward platform expansion. The code stays open, but the center of gravity moves.

## Acquisitions change the map for maintainers

![An open source repository icon being folded into a larger corporate platform diagram, shown as an editorial infographic with subtle tension and clean lines.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/ultimate-jekyll/-OuUKBNEJX9MYzxYZunu/section-3.png)

When a platform buys a popular open source project, it does not just add code to the portfolio. It also inherits roadmap influence, hiring decisions, and the expectations of a community that assumed neutrality. Sometimes that gives the project more runway and better funding. Sometimes it narrows the project’s identity to fit the parent company’s strategy.

For maintainers, that can be a tradeoff worth taking if it preserves momentum and pays the people doing the work. For users, it is a reminder to watch governance, not just GitHub stars. A healthy repo matters, but the actual business model matters more.

---

## What this means for smaller teams

![A small technical team reviewing a deployment checklist beside a calm, modular static site architecture diagram, warm and practical editorial style.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/ultimate-jekyll/-OuUKBNEJX9MYzxYZunu/section-4.png)

If you are building on Jekyll or any other mature static site stack, the lesson is not to panic. It is to choose tools with boring, durable economics. The safest options are the ones with clear maintenance practices, transparent governance, and a deployment path that does not depend on one company’s growth targets.

That usually means favoring simple front matter workflows, predictable plugins, and hosting setups you can move if needed. The more your site depends on a single vendor’s strategic priorities, the more fragile your stack becomes. Stability is a feature, not a compromise.
