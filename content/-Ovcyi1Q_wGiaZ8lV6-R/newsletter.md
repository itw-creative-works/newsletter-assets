# Open models, endless agents, and cheaper software

_What this means for support teams living in Gmail and the browser._

The interesting part of the current AI wave is not that models are smarter. It is that they are starting to act like little operators with stamina.

That changes the job for founders and support teams: less time spent asking whether AI can do the work, more time spent deciding what context it needs to do it well.

---

## Open models are getting good enough to matter

The open-model conversation has shifted from novelty to usefulness. A few years ago, open-weight systems were mostly about tinkering and benchmarks. Now they are showing up with real strengths: native multimodal input, huge context windows, multilingual coverage, and license terms that make them easier to deploy in real products.

That matters for support and operations teams because model choice is becoming less about prestige and more about fit. Some jobs want speed and cost control. Others want long context, better reasoning, or language coverage across regions. The practical move is to think in terms of task-specific lanes, not one model to rule the inbox.

---

## Agents can do more, but only with better context

There is a familiar pattern in agent workflows: they can access the tools, but still do not understand the situation. That is why some automations look impressive in demos and then wobble in production. More connectors and permissions help, but they do not replace a real context layer.

For inbox work, the lesson is painfully practical. An auto-reply system does not just need the customer email. It needs the account history, the product state, prior promises, tone rules, escalation paths, and the list of things it should not say. Without that, the agent is basically a very confident intern with browser access.

## Long-horizon tasks are the new normal

The newer agent features are leaning into persistence. Instead of answering once and disappearing, they keep working until a goal is reached, checking progress and adjusting along the way. That is useful for multi-step jobs like editing, scheduling, or account triage, where the work is not a single prompt but a sequence of decisions.

For support teams, this points to a different kind of automation. Not just replying faster, but running a process: classify, draft, verify, escalate, and follow up. The win is not dramatic autonomy. It is fewer half-finished tasks sitting in the queue because someone got pulled into a meeting.

---

## Cheaper software means more niche software

As AI lowers the cost of building and rewriting software, product ideas get smaller and more specific. That opens the door for domain experts and tiny teams to ship tools aimed at narrow workflows, local rules, and weird edge cases that bigger platforms usually ignore.

For customer support, that is good news. It means more teams will build lightweight systems around their own process instead of trying to force every problem into a generic help desk template. It also means more disposable software, which sounds rude but is mostly accurate: if rebuilding gets cheaper, teams will stop treating every internal tool like a cathedral.
