# Merch is getting smarter, cheaper, and easier to scan

_From recycled materials to better team scorecards, here’s what’s changing_

Merch is not just getting a facelift. It is being tied to smarter materials, better customer experiences, and stronger measurement. For brands, that means every printed asset should work harder, look better, and prove its value.

---

## The physical object is now part of the funnel

![A modern branded tote bag, apparel tag, and product insert laid out on a desk with stylish custom QR codes integrated into the design, editorial illustration style.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/dashqr/-Ov94AWVk3JL_viTlC4D/section-1.png)

A shirt, bag, insert card, or product tag used to be a branding asset. Now it can also be a conversion step. That is where custom QR codes earn their place: they turn something people already hold into something measurable. The best merch does not just look good on a table or in a mailer. It gives someone a reason to scan, and it makes the next step obvious, whether that is a landing page, signup form, discount code, or event check-in. If the scan experience is clean and on-brand, the printed item starts pulling real weight instead of just taking up space.

---

## Better design increases the chance of the scan

![A premium product package and business card mockup with a beautifully integrated colored QR code and logo, clean studio lighting, minimalist design.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/dashqr/-Ov94AWVk3JL_viTlC4D/section-2.png)

A QR code should not feel like an afterthought slapped onto packaging at the last minute. When it matches the brand palette, uses a logo carefully, and has enough contrast to scan reliably, it feels like part of the design system. That matters because people are more likely to interact with something that looks intentional. For packaging, think about placement near the main callout. For flyers or menus, keep the code close to the decision point. For business cards, make the scan benefit obvious. The goal is simple: make the code useful enough that it earns space and attractive enough that it does not ruin the layout.

## Dynamic codes are a merchandising superpower

![A stack of branded flyers and packaging with one QR code highlighted as a living digital portal, shown as a clean infographic-style illustration.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/dashqr/-Ov94AWVk3JL_viTlC4D/section-3.png)

One of the most practical parts of dynamic QR codes is that they let you change the destination without reprinting anything. That is especially useful for merch runs, seasonal packaging, or event materials that need to stay relevant after the first batch goes out. You can swap a promo page for a signup form, update a restaurant menu, or route scans by location. For small teams, that flexibility saves money. For larger campaigns, it protects against dead ends. Instead of treating print as permanent, you treat it like a living entry point that can adapt to inventory, offers, or timing.

---

## Measurement is where print stops guessing

![A dashboard view beside printed flyers, packaging, and menu cards, with scan heatmaps and line charts in a polished brand analytics illustration.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/dashqr/-Ov94AWVk3JL_viTlC4D/section-4.png)

If you cannot see what is working, you are guessing. Scan analytics make it possible to compare placement, timing, and creative choices across different printed assets. A flyer at checkout might outperform a poster on a wall. A menu code near the top right may scan more than one buried in the footer. A QR on a shipping insert might drive more repeat purchases than a code on packaging. With the right tracking, you can test those assumptions and keep the winners. That is the real upgrade: not just prettier merch, but merch that teaches you how customers behave in the offline world.

## Sustainability and efficiency are starting to align

![An eco-conscious packaging concept with recycled paper textures, a custom QR code, and subtle green design accents, rendered in a clean premium illustration style.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/dashqr/-Ov94AWVk3JL_viTlC4D/section-5.png)

Brands are also under pressure to do more with less material waste, whether that means using recycled inputs, simplifying print runs, or reducing the need to reprint outdated assets. That is where a smarter QR strategy helps. If a code can be updated after printing, you can extend the life of a piece instead of throwing it away when the offer changes. Less rework, fewer obsolete inserts, more flexibility. The sustainability angle is not just about materials. It is also about avoiding waste in the customer journey by making each physical asset adaptable, trackable, and worth keeping in circulation longer.
