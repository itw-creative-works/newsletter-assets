500 issues in, the patterns are clear

Five hundred issues is long enough to spot the real center of gravity in developer tooling. The headline is not that everything changed at once, but that the most durable topics kept drifting toward orchestration, automation, and the unglamorous plumbing underneath modern software.

The boring topics are the durable ones
Teams do not spend most of their time on novelty, they spend it on compatibility, build speed, runtime behavior, and the endless edge cases that show up once real traffic hits.

If you only skim product launches, it is easy to miss where developer attention actually sticks. The long-running winners are still the same fundamentals: the web platform, React, AI agents, and the tooling around TypeScript and bundlers. That makes sense in production. The pattern is familiar in proxy infrastructure too: the value is usually in reliable routing, clean retries, and consistent fingerprints, not in whatever sounds exciting on a demo stage.

Agents are moving from helpers to systems
The unit is the whole retry path, the rotation logic, the validation step, and the recovery plan when the first attempt gets blocked.

The most interesting shift in tooling is not another chatbot in the terminal. It is the move toward systems that can triage, implement, review, verify, and monitor work with less hand-holding. That changes the shape of the tool itself. Instead of one prompt, one answer, teams need orchestration across multiple models, isolated environments, and verification loops that can catch the usual failures before they escape into production. For backend and scraping workflows, that same mindset applies: one request is not the unit of work anymore.

CLI tools are getting more competitive, not less
Now they are competing with heavyweight model-backed products from the biggest vendors, which means the standalone tools have to win on experience, extensibility, and trust.

Terminal-first products used to have the advantage of being the default path for builders. That is no longer true. Open sourcing core pieces is one way to keep developers close while the platform evolves. The deeper lesson is that command-line workflows are not disappearing, they are being redefined around integrations, model choice, and automation hooks that let the terminal act more like an operations layer than a text box.

Milestones are a good excuse to audit your assumptions
It is a chance to look at what kept showing up, what faded away, and what you probably should have taken more seriously earlier.

Reaching a big issue count is not just a vanity metric. In developer tooling, that kind of retrospective is useful because the landscape changes faster than most people admit. The stacks that survive are the ones that make hard jobs easier under real constraints: flaky networks, blocked requests, weird browser fingerprints, and users in different regions. Whether you are shipping a proxy API or an agent platform, the winning move is usually the same, build for the messy middle, not the clean demo.
