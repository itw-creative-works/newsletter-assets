# The new social playbook is about tension

_From TikTok's ad expansion to brands winning by surprising their category._

The smartest social teams are leaning into two shifts at once: platforms are opening up more inventory, and audiences are responding to brands that feel unexpectedly human, cinematic, or niche in the right ways. That combination is changing how growth looks across paid and organic.

---

## TikTok is widening the ad surface

![A clean, modern illustration of a social media dashboard expanding into multiple ad placements on a phone screen, with bold motion lines and a marketing team reviewing creative variants.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/somiibo/-OscYjdHG2wEk09U-D3G/section-1.png)

TikTok’s ad opportunity is no longer limited to the obvious placements most teams started with. As the platform broadens where ads can appear and how they can be bought, the practical takeaway is simple: creative and targeting have to work harder together.

For marketers, this is less about chasing every new format and more about building a system that can adapt quickly. Short-form creative still matters, but so does testing multiple hooks, offers, and audience angles. The brands that benefit most will be the ones that treat TikTok like a flexible distribution engine, not a single ad format.

---

## The best social accounts create tension

![A split-scene illustration showing three contrasting brand archetypes colliding into one social feed: luxury, finance, and public institution, rendered in a polished editorial style.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/somiibo/-OscYjdHG2wEk09U-D3G/section-2.png)

A strong social presence often comes from doing the thing people would not expect from your category. Luxury brands can borrow from internet-native creator culture, fintech brands can be playful without losing authority, and legacy institutions can sound surprisingly current if the execution is thoughtful.

That tension works because it gives people something to pause on. Instead of blending into the category feed, the brand becomes a pattern break. The key is not randomness — it is contrast with a purpose. When the voice, format, or visual style clearly clashes with audience expectations in a smart way, attention follows.

## Cinematic content is becoming a brand differentiator

![A cinematic social video still with dramatic lighting, a bold title card, and a sports arena backdrop, styled like an editorial poster.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/somiibo/-OscYjdHG2wEk09U-D3G/section-3.png)

More brands are leaning into cinematic editing, story structure, and visual language usually reserved for film trailers or music videos. That style can feel fresh because it slows the scroll in a feed full of disposable-looking clips.

The point is not to make everything feel expensive. It is to make the audience feel something specific: anticipation, nostalgia, humor, or scale. A well-framed title card, a deliberate color palette, or a more dramatic reveal can instantly change how a campaign is perceived. For content teams, the lesson is to stop thinking only in terms of volume and start thinking in terms of visual point of view.

## LinkedIn is past its cringe era

![An editorial-style illustration of a professional creator posting on LinkedIn from a laptop, with floating content cards showing tips, charts, and short-form insights.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/somiibo/-OscYjdHG2wEk09U-D3G/section-4.png)

LinkedIn has quietly become one of the most useful places to build an audience if your content is clear, useful, and consistent. The platform now rewards people who can teach, summarize, and translate their expertise into formats that feel native to the feed.

That opens the door for creators, founders, and marketers who used to ignore it. The winning play is not to force a personal-brand voice into every post. It is to identify what your audience wants to learn from you and package it in a way that is easy to scan, easy to trust, and easy to remember. In other words: utility beats polish.

---

## Audience growth now favors format fit

![A strategic visual of a content planner mapping stories to different platform icons, with notes about tone, pacing, and audience expectations on a desk.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/somiibo/-OscYjdHG2wEk09U-D3G/section-5.png)

The bigger lesson across platforms is that growth is less about posting more and more about matching the right story to the right environment. A campaign that works on one platform can fall flat on another if the tone, pacing, or cultural reference points are off.

That means teams should build with distribution in mind from the start. Ask what the audience expects from the category, then decide where to break that expectation. Whether you are running paid campaigns or organic content, the strongest results usually come from a clear point of view paired with a format people already understand.
