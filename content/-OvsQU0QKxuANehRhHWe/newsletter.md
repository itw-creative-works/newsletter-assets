# The new stack for agentic work

_Search, review, and context are becoming the real product._

There is a quiet pattern showing up in the way people use AI at work: the winning setup is less about a smarter model and more about better scaffolding around the work. Search, review, context, and handoffs are becoming the real leverage points.

Here’s what that looks like in practice for teams that live in code, docs, and repeating workflows.

---

## Why agent tools need guardrails, not just prompts

A lot of teams still judge AI search and retrieval with a few happy-path queries, then wonder why the system falls apart in real use. The better approach is to test against a golden set of queries that reflects actual work, then measure accuracy before anything reaches the team. That same mindset applies to agentic engineering more broadly: if the model cannot reliably find the right context, it will invent it. Good systems make the easy path the correct path, whether that means better retrieval, clearer instructions, or a way to check answers before they ship.

---

## The real productivity gain is the loop around the model

The most useful agent setups are starting to look like workflows, not one-off prompts. Think: an agent drafts work, another pass checks it, and a final step pushes only what survives review. Some builders are even mixing models so one handles generation and another catches mistakes. That may sound fussy, but it is usually what turns an impressive demo into something you can trust every day. The lesson for busy teams is simple: if the loop is weak, the model ends up doing all the work and still missing the edge cases.

## Cross-repo context is becoming a first-class problem

For teams with code split across multiple repositories, AI work breaks down fast when the agent cannot see the whole picture. Monorepos solve part of that by keeping context close, but many orgs cannot or will not reorganize their entire codebase just for AI. That is where context layers and harnesses start to matter. The useful idea here is not that every team needs a monorepo overnight. It is that autonomous work depends on the agent being able to reach the files, services, and decisions that shape the task. Without that, people end up back in the middle as human routers.

---

## Platform work still needs storytelling

Platform PMs have one of the hardest jobs in product because so much of the work is invisible until it fails. They have to serve multiple audiences, manage dependencies they do not fully control, and prove value long after the ticket is closed. That means the job is partly operational and partly narrative. The teams that do this well turn invisible improvements into visible metrics, then translate those metrics for engineering, leadership, and end users without losing the thread. In an AI-heavy workflow, that skill matters even more because the tooling may be new, but the need for adoption, measurement, and trust is the same.
