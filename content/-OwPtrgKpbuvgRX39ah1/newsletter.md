# The simple sequence behind better SEO and AI

_What founders can borrow from SEO, model evals, and portable context_

The biggest mistake I keep seeing in growth and AI work is the same one: people jump to tools before they define the sequence. Whether you are trying to rank, automate support, or improve on-site conversion, the winning move is usually to set the workflow first and the software second.

---

## Start with the right target, not the tool

![A founder at a desk mapping business goals to a simple workflow board, clean editorial illustration, muted brand-friendly colors](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/chatsy/-OwPtrgKpbuvgRX39ah1/section-1.png)

A lot of teams treat SEO like a plugin problem or a chatbot like a feature toggle. In practice, the better question is what you actually have a right to win. A niche business should not chase generic, high-volume terms if the audience is too broad or the intent is wrong. The same logic applies to support automation: do not try to automate everything at once. Pick the tickets, questions, or lead paths that map to clear business value. If you can name the outcome, you can choose the channel, the content, and the handoff. If you cannot, you are just creating busywork with better software.

## Portable context beats starting from scratch

![A laptop screen showing a plain markdown file beside multiple AI app icons, modern flat illustration with a practical workspace feel](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/chatsy/-OwPtrgKpbuvgRX39ah1/section-2.png)

One of the most useful habits in AI work is keeping your context outside any single app. If your prompts, customer details, brand voice, and edge cases only live inside one tool, you are one outage away from redoing everything. Save the essentials in plain text or markdown so any model can pick them up. For support teams, that means reusable instructions for tone, escalation rules, product facts, and common objections. For marketing teams, it means a lightweight brief that keeps every draft aligned. The goal is not perfection. It is portability, so your work survives tool changes and your workflow does not depend on memory.

## Measure the response, not just the output

![A dashboard with simple success metrics, a chatbot conversation, and a conversion funnel, illustrated in a clean SaaS analytics style](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/chatsy/-OwPtrgKpbuvgRX39ah1/section-3.png)

If you want better content, better bots, or better ads, measure whether people do the next thing you intended. A reel is not successful because it got views. A chatbot is not successful because it replied quickly. A support flow is successful when it deflects the right tickets, moves people to the right answer, or helps them trust the result enough to act on it. That means choosing the metric before you ship. For a bot, it might be resolved conversations, fewer repetitive tickets, or higher lead capture from high-intent visitors. Without that upfront decision, every result looks vaguely good and nothing gets improved.

## Test AI like a system, not a vibe

![A side-by-side AI evaluation board with scored outputs and checklists, styled like a product review lab](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/chatsy/-OwPtrgKpbuvgRX39ah1/section-4.png)

When people evaluate AI tools with a few random prompts, they usually end up judging their own mood more than the model. A better approach is a repeatable test set. Use the same prompts, the same scoring criteria, and the same comparison process each time. Mix human judgment with structured scoring so you catch both quality and usefulness. This is especially helpful for customer-facing bots, where tone matters almost as much as accuracy. If a bot sounds smart but feels off-brand, it is still a bad fit. A simple benchmark turns AI selection from a debate into a process you can actually trust.
