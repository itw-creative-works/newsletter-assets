SEO works best when you stop starting with keywords
Most teams begin SEO by asking what they should rank for, then build content around that answer.

That is usually backwards. The better approach is to start with the business slice you can actually win, then map keywords to that niche, not the other way around. After that, fix the technical basics, define which pages deserve attention, and only then scale content production. For a support or sales team, the practical lesson is the same: do not publish content just because it is easy to generate. Build pages that answer the exact questions your buyers ask before they convert or submit a ticket. Sequence beats speed when the goal is durable traffic.

Model evals are getting more useful, but only if you test like a system
A better pattern is to run repeatable tests across the tasks that matter most, like drafting specs, finishing workflows, and matching your team’s tone.

One-off AI comparisons are fun, but they rarely help you make a decision next month. It also helps to mix human judgment with model-based scoring instead of trusting either one alone. That matters for customer support too. If you are evaluating a chatbot, test for usefulness, tone, and whether the output can be used as written, not just whether it sounds impressive. The goal is not finding the flashiest model. It is finding the one that performs consistently on your actual workload.

Portable context is the antidote to AI lock-in
The practical fix is portable context: keep your key preferences, project notes, and reusable instructions in plain files that any tool can read.

A lot of AI frustration comes from losing the history that made one tool feel smart in the first place. That way, if your main app goes down or you want to try another model, you are not starting from zero. For SMB teams, this is especially useful for support bots and internal assistants. Store your product facts, tone rules, escalation logic, and common objections in a simple format your whole stack can reuse. Context should travel with the work, not live inside one vendor.

Local AI is moving from hobby project to real option
Now it is becoming a serious choice for teams that care about control, privacy, and infrastructure flexibility.

Running models on your own machine used to sound like a niche experiment. Smaller open models are getting good enough for more workloads, and that changes the conversation from “can we do this locally?” to “what should we keep local?” For customer support, that could mean routing sensitive workflows to private systems while using hosted tools for lower-risk tasks. You do not need to move everything off the cloud. But it is worth thinking in layers: what needs speed, what needs privacy, and what needs human review.

AI product metrics should measure trust, not just usage
If you are shipping an AI feature, clicks alone will not tell you whether it is working.

You want to know whether people use the output as delivered, edit it less, verify it less often, and trust it with more important tasks over time. Those are the signals that show real value. This is especially useful for support automation. A chatbot that gets plenty of traffic but still sends every visitor to a human is not saving much labor. The better metric is whether it resolves more questions end to end and helps people move faster without second-guessing the result. Measure behavior, not just activity.
