In this issue
 

AI agents don’t fail at demo time. They fail later.

Everyone loves a clean demo. The problem is that inboxes, support queues, and internal workflows do not behave like demo environments.

The useful question is not whether an agent can answer. It is whether it can keep answering, route well, and leave behind enough signal to improve next week.

The demo is the easy part
Deployment is what happens when the same system has to handle edge cases, missing context, angry customers, and the 47th follow-up on the same thread.

A lot of AI agents get built around a single impressive moment: they answer one question, draft one reply, or complete one task. That is not deployment. The companies getting value out of agents are the ones treating them like part of operations, not like a science project. They design for the boring stuff first: permissions, escalation paths, and what happens when the model is unsure. That is where the real work lives, which is unfortunate but consistent with how software usually behaves.

Structure beats improvisation
On social, that means auditing what already performs, then turning winning patterns into reusable formats.

Fast-moving teams are learning that AI works better when the workflow is structured before the model shows up. In support, it means defining reply templates, intent buckets, and escalation rules before letting an agent loose on the inbox. The same logic applies across channels: if you want scale, the system needs to know what counts as a sales question, a bug report, or a billing issue. AI is good at filling in gaps. It is much better when the gaps are already framed. That is less glamorous than “autonomous intelligence,” but it tends to ship.

Measure the outcome, not the novelty
If you only measure whether it sounded smart, you will miss whether it actually reduced response time or improved customer sentiment.

The next mistake is treating an agent like a feature instead of a process. Teams need to watch a few blunt numbers: first response time, escalation rate, repeat contact rate, and whether the tone matches the brand or sounds like it swallowed a help center. A useful agent should leave a paper trail. Which intents keep recurring? Which replies get reopened? Which customers still need a human after the first pass? If you cannot answer those questions, the agent is mostly theater with extra steps.

Even the giants are still figuring out the plumbing
That means authentication, access controls, memory, and observability matter as much as model quality.

The broader AI market keeps signaling ambition, but ambition is not the same thing as operating discipline. Big product launches and confidential filing chatter may grab headlines, yet the durable story is more basic: teams are racing to turn AI into infrastructure, not just a headline. If an agent cannot log in safely, remember the right context, and show its work, it is not ready for a serious inbox. The companies that win here will not be the ones with the loudest demo. They will be the ones who made the system hard to break.
