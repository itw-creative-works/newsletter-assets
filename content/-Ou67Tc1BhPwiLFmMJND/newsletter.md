# Claude enters Microsoft 365 workflows

_A quieter AI upgrade: faster docs, cleaner decks, less inbox friction._

Claude is moving closer to the daily grind of office work, which matters more than another flashy demo. If your team lives in Excel, PowerPoint, and email, the real win is shaving friction off the boring steps that eat the afternoon.

---

## The useful AI is the one sitting inside the workflow

![A clean modern office desk with a laptop showing Microsoft 365 apps and an AI assistant panel, illustrated in a muted editorial style.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/replyify/-Ou67Tc1BhPwiLFmMJND/section-1.png)

The best AI update is usually the one that does not ask people to learn a new habit. Putting Claude into Microsoft 365 means the model can sit closer to the files, decks, and drafts your team already handles. That matters for support teams too, because the same pattern applies to inbox work: fewer tab switches, fewer copy-paste loops, less “I’ll do it later” energy. If AI can turn a spreadsheet or doc into a first pass faster, the human job becomes review, polish, and judgment. That is where the actual value lives.

---

## Presentations are still mostly formatting pain

![An illustrated presentation deck being assembled from a spreadsheet and document files, with slide thumbnails snapping into place.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/replyify/-Ou67Tc1BhPwiLFmMJND/section-2.png)

One of the most practical use cases is taking a file and turning it into a presentation without starting from a blank slide deck like it is 2009. Uploading a spreadsheet, CSV, or other data file and getting a structured deck back saves time on the most annoying part of presentation work: making the first draft look like you meant it. The same logic applies to customer support summaries and internal updates. AI is not replacing the thinking. It is replacing the part where someone spends 45 minutes aligning text boxes and pretending that counts as strategy.

## Inbox teams should care about response speed, not model drama

![A support inbox dashboard beside an AI reply draft and analytics panel, shown as a crisp product illustration with subtle motion.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/replyify/-Ou67Tc1BhPwiLFmMJND/section-3.png)

For support and operations teams, the big question is not which model has the prettiest benchmark score. It is whether the tool helps you answer faster without sounding canned. If a model can draft a useful reply from company data, the next step is measuring what changes: first-response time, reopen rates, and whether customers feel heard. That is the practical frame for any AI rollout. You are not buying cleverness. You are buying fewer repeats, shorter queues, and a cleaner handoff between the messy inbox and the person responsible for making sense of it.

---

## The small-team advantage is still context

![A small team in a compact workspace reviewing AI-generated email drafts on a large screen, with an emphasis on practical collaboration.](https://raw.githubusercontent.com/itw-creative-works/newsletter-assets/main/replyify/-Ou67Tc1BhPwiLFmMJND/section-4.png)

The teams that get the most out of AI usually are not the ones with the biggest budgets. They are the ones with the clearest internal context. When a system learns from your own docs, policies, and past replies, it becomes less generic and more useful. That is especially true for founders and solo operators who cannot afford to babysit every message. The goal is not a perfectly autonomous inbox. It is a decent first draft, sent faster, with enough context that your replies still sound like a person wrote them after coffee.
