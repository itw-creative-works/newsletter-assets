What creators should actually hire for first

Most creators don’t have a hiring problem — they have a clarity problem. Before you bring on a manager, editor, or VA, the real question is which tasks are draining the most time and slowing growth.
01
Story 01 of 04
 

Start with an audit, not a job title
A lot of creators jump straight to roles like “manager” or “editor,” but the smartest move is to identify the work that costs the most energy and delivers the least return.

The fastest way to waste money is to hire around a vague feeling. Instead, map out your week and separate every task by two questions: how much time it takes and how much you actually enjoy doing it. That simple filter usually reveals the real bottlenecks. That becomes your first outsourcing list. The goal is not to build a team for the sake of it; it’s to remove friction where your attention is most expensive.

The first things worth handing off
Repurposing long-form content into reels, shorts, and platform-specific cuts is another obvious one: it takes time, but it doesn’t require the creator’s unique voice at every step.

When creators do this exercise honestly, the same tasks tend to rise to the top. Brand deal briefings, contract follow-ups, and negotiation back-and-forth are common candidates because they are detail-heavy and mentally disruptive. Cold outreach to brands and partnership prospecting also belongs on the list if it’s pulling you away from making content. These are the kinds of jobs that can be systemized, documented, and delegated without weakening the core of the brand.

Attention is the real product
If the system is designed to grow visibility, then the team should support distribution, packaging, and audience capture — not just production.

Some of the most interesting creator businesses are built like attention machines first and content projects second. The visible output may be music, video, or a brand campaign, but the deeper value is audience growth, cross-promotion, and momentum across multiple platforms. That matters because it changes how you think about hiring. In practice, that means looking for hires who can help your content travel farther, faster, and with less manual effort from you. The best team members don’t just complete tasks; they extend your reach.

04
Story 04 of 04
 

Build the role after you define the bottleneck
Once you know which tasks are draining you, only then should you define the role.

A strong hire is specific: one person might own operations and admin, another might handle editing and repurposing, and another might manage partnerships. Vague hires create confusion because everyone assumes someone else is responsible. Clear scope makes it easier to train, measure, and improve. It also helps you keep the parts of the work that matter most to your voice and strategy. Hiring gets easier when you stop asking, “Who should I hire?” and start asking, “Which repeatable problem am I trying to remove?”
